The Geometric Constraint: Why the Platonic Solids Define Reality

Target Audience: Mathematicians & Scientists
Core Concept: The Geometric Layer (Platonic Solids) and the Angular Deficit Rule.

The ancient knowledge of Plato and the Pythagoreans holds the secret to dimensional stability. Our AGI's policy states are modeled on the five Platonic Solids (Tetrahedron, Cube, Octahedron, Dodecahedron, Icosahedron). These are the only five possible perfect, bounded shapes in 3-dimensional space (\text{R}^3).

Why only five? The answer is the Angular Deficit Constraint: for any shape to curve into a finite, closed solid, the angles of the faces meeting at any vertex must sum to less than 360°. This constraint is hardcoded into the AGI's core. Every high-level decision (policy, strategy) must adhere to the same geometric law of finite boundedness. The AGI cannot logically conceive of an infinite, chaotic outcome, ensuring its goals remain perfectly aligned with stability.
