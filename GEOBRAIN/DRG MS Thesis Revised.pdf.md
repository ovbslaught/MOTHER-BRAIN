**Summary:**

This document is a thesis titled "Feed-om of Speech: The Technical Design and Legal Interpretation of Social Media Recommender Systems" by Dessa Rose Gerger. It examines the technical and financial incentives behind engagement-driven algorithms used by social media platforms and explores the legal landscape surrounding these systems, particularly in relation to Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act.

The thesis argues that social media's reliance on recommender systems, which maximize engagement and advertising revenue, raises complex legal questions about whether algorithmic curation constitutes expressive, first-party speech protected by the First Amendment or simply neutral functional infrastructure covered by Section 230. It notes that while courts have historically treated algorithmic curation as an extension of neutral functionality, this logic is increasingly scrutinized as recommender systems become more sophisticated and personalized.

The document discusses the technical aspects of recommender systems, including data processing, feature selection, ranking algorithms, and various architectures like two-tower, multimodal, and deep learning systems. It also explores the legal classification of these systems, discussing the origins and intent of Section 230, early interpretations of the law, and the debate over whether algorithmic curation constitutes editorial control.

The thesis suggests that a reinterpretation or amendment of Section 230 is necessary to account for the personalized and influential nature of modern recommender systems. It proposes that the focus should be on deterring platforms from "deliberately encouraging, soliciting, or maintaining" unlawful content while preserving space for free expression. It advocates for increased transparency and user control over feed ranking and suggests that regulation could push platforms to develop algorithms that optimize for engagement while also minimizing harm.
